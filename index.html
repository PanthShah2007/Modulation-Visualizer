Glossary & formulas

Every term, symbol, unit, and formula used in the visualizer — explained

Why modulation exists in the first place: a message signal (like audio or data) usually has a very low frequency, which means it needs an impractically large antenna to transmit efficiently, travels only short distances, and cannot be separated from other signals sharing the same medium. Modulation solves all three problems by "riding" the message on top of a much higher-frequency carrier wave — this shrinks the required antenna size, extends transmission range, and allows many signals to share the same channel by simply giving each one a different carrier frequency (this is how radio stations don't interfere with each other).
Core signals
Message signal m(t) Volts (V), function of time
Also called the baseband signal or modulating signal. This is the actual information you want to send — voice, music, sensor data, a video feed, etc. In this visualizer it's simplified to a single sine wave m(t) = Am · cos(2π·fm·t) so the effect of modulation is easy to see, but in real systems it's a complex, irregular waveform. It's called "baseband" because its frequency content sits near zero Hz, which is exactly why it can't be transmitted efficiently on its own.
Carrier signal c(t) Volts (V), function of time
A pure, high-frequency sine wave c(t) = Ac · cos(2π·fc·t) that carries no information by itself — its only job is to act as a "vehicle" for the message signal. Carrier frequencies are chosen far higher than the message frequency (radio carriers run in the kHz–GHz range) because higher frequency waves need much smaller antennas and travel further with less power loss.
Modulated signal / output s(t) Volts (V), function of time
The result of combining the message and carrier — this is what actually gets transmitted through the antenna or channel. Depending on which property of the carrier you vary (amplitude, frequency, or phase) in proportion to the message signal, you get AM, FM, or PM respectively. At the receiver, a demodulator reverses this process to recover the original message signal.
Parameters (the sliders)
Carrier frequency fc Hertz (Hz)
How many oscillations per second the carrier wave makes. This is fixed by the transmission band you're allocated (e.g. FM radio sits around 88–108 MHz). In the visualizer, increasing fc packs more carrier cycles into the same time window, so the modulated output looks "denser."
Message frequency fm Hertz (Hz)
How fast the information signal itself oscillates. For this to work properly, fc must always be much greater than fm — if they get close, the carrier can no longer cleanly "carry" the message, and demodulation at the receiver becomes unreliable.
Modulation index m (AM) or β (FM/PM) Unitless (ratio)
Controls how strongly the message signal affects the carrier — essentially the "depth" of modulation.
  • AM: m = Am / Ac — the ratio of message amplitude to carrier amplitude. m < 1 gives clean modulation; m > 1 causes "overmodulation" and distortion at the receiver.
  • FM: β = Δf / fm — ratio of peak frequency deviation to message frequency. Higher β spreads the signal energy across more sidebands, using more bandwidth but improving noise immunity.
  • PM: β directly represents the peak phase deviation in radians.
Mode AM / FM / PM —
Which property of the carrier the message signal is allowed to vary.
The three modulation types
Amplitude modulation AM
The message signal varies the amplitude of the carrier while its frequency and phase stay constant. The message appears as an "envelope" tracing the outer shape of the modulated wave.
s(t) = Ac · [1 + m·cos(2π·fm·t)] · cos(2π·fc·t)
Simple to implement and demodulate, but more sensitive to noise since noise also affects amplitude. Used in AM radio (medium wave band).
Frequency modulation FM
The message signal varies the instantaneous frequency of the carrier while amplitude stays constant. Where the message is at its peak, the carrier oscillates faster; where the message is at its trough, the carrier oscillates slower.
s(t) = Ac · cos(2π·fc·t + β·sin(2π·fm·t))
Much more resistant to amplitude-based noise (static), which is why FM radio sounds clearer than AM. Uses more bandwidth than AM.
Phase modulation PM
The message signal varies the phase (the horizontal shift/timing) of the carrier while amplitude stays constant.
s(t) = Ac · cos(2π·fc·t + β·cos(2π·fm·t))
Closely related to FM mathematically — phase and frequency are linked since frequency is the rate of change of phase. PM is the foundation for digital schemes like PSK (Phase Shift Keying) used in Wi-Fi and satellite communication.
